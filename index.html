Foundations of Clinical Surgery

Welcome to your surgical journey! Remember: 70% of diagnosis comes from history, 20% from examination, and only 10% from investigations. Make your patient talk, listen carefully, and always examine thoroughly. This is the art and science of surgery!

Theoretical Lecture: History Taking

The Three Pillars of Diagnosis:

Pillar Contribution Description
History (Hx) 70% Subjective information from patient in their own words
Examination (Exam) 20% Objective observations and physical findings
Investigations (Inv) 10% Confirm diagnosis & rule out differentials

Components of Comprehensive History:

  1. Personal History: Name, Age, Sex, Occupation, Residence, Marital status, Special habits, Source of information
  2. Chief Complaint (CC): Patient's own words, always include duration
  3. History of Present Illness (HPI):
    • When was the patient last well?
    • Detailed analysis of the complaint (OPQRST)
    • Associated symptoms
    • Constitutional symptoms (fever, weight loss, appetite)
    • Complete review of systems
  4. Past Medical History: Similar conditions, chronic diseases, medications, allergies
  5. Past Surgical History: Previous operations, complications, blood transfusions
  6. Family History: Similar conditions, consanguinity, hereditary diseases
  7. Socioeconomic History: Housing, water supply, animal contact
Clinical Pearl: Always perform a complete Review of Systems (ROS) even with localized complaints to detect complications, generate differential diagnoses, and assess fitness for surgery.

Wound Care & Infection Control

The surgeon is not just the one who cuts, but the one who knows how to heal! Wound healing is a complex physiological miracle. Remember: a clean wound is a healing wound. Infection is your enemy - prioritize sterilization, debridement, and timely closure!

Skin Anatomy & Physiology

Three Primary Layers:

  1. Epidermis (Outer Layer):
    • Stratified squamous epithelium
    • No blood vessels (avascular)
    • Contains: Keratinocytes, Melanocytes, Langerhans cells
    • 5 sublayers: Basale â†’ Spinosum â†’ Granulosum â†’ Lucidum â†’ Corneum
  2. Dermis (Middle Layer):
    • Contains blood vessels, nerves, glands, hair follicles
    • Collagen and elastic fibers for strength
    • Two regions: Papillary and Reticular dermis
  3. Subcutaneous Tissue (Deepest):
    • Fatty layer (adipose tissue)
    • Insulation, energy storage, cushioning
    • Contains large blood vessels and nerves

Functions of Skin:

  • Protection: Mechanical barrier against pathogens
  • Thermoregulation: Sweating, vasodilation/constriction
  • Sensation: Touch, pressure, temperature, pain
  • Metabolism: Vitamin D synthesis
  • Excretion: Waste products in sweat

Major Skin Barriers:

  1. Continuous desquamation of stratum corneum
  2. Epithelium as mechanical barrier
  3. Lysozyme in sweat and sebum
  4. Bacteriocins produced by normal flora

Phases of Wound Healing

1. Inflammatory Phase (0-5 days):

  • Hemostasis: Platelet aggregation, fibrin clot formation
  • Vascular Response: Vasoconstriction â†’ Vasodilation
  • Cellular Response:
    • Neutrophils (first 24-48h): Phagocytosis of bacteria
    • Macrophages (from day 2): Key regulators, release growth factors
    • Lymphocytes: Immune modulation
  • Mediators: Histamine, serotonin, kinins, cytokines

2. Proliferative Phase (Day 3-21):

  • Granulation Tissue: Fibroblasts, new vessels, collagen
  • Angiogenesis: New capillary formation (starts day 3-4)
  • Collagen Synthesis: Type III collagen predominant
  • Epithelialization: Re-epithelialization from wound edges
  • Wound Contraction: Myofibroblasts reduce wound size

3. Maturation (Remodeling) Phase (Week 3-12):

  • Collagen III â†’ Collagen I conversion
  • Increased tensile strength (up to 80% of original)
  • Scar maturation and flattening
  • Apoptosis of excess cells
Wound Strength Timeline: 20% at 3 weeks â†’ 50% at 6 weeks â†’ 80% at 3 months

Wound Closure Types

1. Primary Closure (First Intention):

  • All layers closed immediately after surgery
  • Indications: Clean wounds, minimal contamination
  • Advantages: Rapid healing, minimal scar
  • Timing: Within 6-8 hours (extremities) or 12 hours (face)

2. Secondary Closure (Second Intention):

  • Wound left open to heal by granulation
  • Indications: Infected wounds, contaminated wounds, tissue loss
  • Process: Contracture and epithelialization from edges
  • Results in larger scar but lower infection risk

3. Tertiary Closure (Delayed Primary):

  • Wound left open for 3-5 days, then closed
  • Indications: Contaminated wounds, questionable viability
  • Advantages: Allows drainage, observe for infection
  • Reduces infection rates compared to primary closure

4. Skin Grafting:

  • Split-thickness: Epidermis + partial dermis
  • Full-thickness: Entire dermis, better cosmetic result

Wound Infection

Surgical Site Infection (SSI) Definition:

Occurring within 30 days after surgery, involving skin, subcutaneous tissue, organ space, or deep tissues.

Risk Factors:

Patient-Related:
  • Age >60 years (immunosenescence)
  • Obesity (BMI >30)
  • Diabetes mellitus (hyperglycemia)
  • Malnutrition (albumin <3.5 g/dl)
  • Smoking (impaired tissue oxygenation)
  • Immunosuppression (HIV, steroids, chemotherapy)
  • Remote infection (nasal MRSA carriage)
Surgery-Related:
  • Duration >2 hours
  • Emergency surgery
  • Break in sterile technique
  • Foreign body placement (prosthesis, mesh)
  • Hypothermia (<36Â°C)
  • Hypoxia, hypotension
  • Prolonged pre-op hospitalization (>48h)

Common Pathogens by Site:

Site Pathogens Prophylaxis
Skin/Soft Tissue Staph. aureus, Strep. pyogenes Cefazolin 1-2g IV
Colorectal E. coli, Bacteroides, Enterococci Cefoxitin + Metronidazole
Biliary E. coli, Klebsiella, Enterococcus Cefazolin or Ampicillin-Sulbactam
Gynecologic Anaerobes, Gram-negative bacilli Cefoxitin

Signs of Wound Infection:

  • Cardinal Signs: Calor (heat), Dolor (pain), Rubor (redness), Tumor (swelling)
  • Purulent drainage: Yellow, green, or brown discharge
  • Systemic signs: Fever, tachycardia, malaise
  • Delayed healing: Wound breakdown, dehiscence
Treatment Principles: Open infected wounds, obtain cultures, start empirical antibiotics (cover MRSA if suspected), debride necrotic tissue, consider NPWT.

Complications of Wound Healing

Early Complications:
  • Infection (most common)
  • Hematoma/Seroma formation
  • Wound dehiscence (breakdown)
  • Skin necrosis
  • Hypersensitivity reaction
Late Complications:
  • Hypertrophic scar
  • Keloid formation
  • Contracture formation
  • Incisional hernia
  • Marjolin's ulcer (SCC in chronic wound)

Hypertrophic Scar vs Keloid:

Feature Hypertrophic Scar Keloid
Growth Pattern Confined to wound boundaries Extends beyond wound margins
Timeline Develops 3-6 months, may regress Develops months-years, persists
Incidence Common, all races Genetic predisposition, darker skin
Treatment Pressure therapy, silicone sheets Steroid injection, excision+RT
Recurrence Rare High without adjuvant therapy
Prevention: Minimize tension on wound edges, use subcuticular sutures, apply silicone gel/sheets early, and consider pressure garments for high-risk areas.

Wound Management Principles

Acute Wound Management:

  1. Assessment: Determine mechanism, depth, contamination, neurovascular status
  2. Hemostasis: Direct pressure, tourniquet if life-threatening
  3. Prevention: Tetanus prophylaxis (Td booster if >5 years)
  4. Antibiotics: Prophylaxis for contaminated wounds, high-risk patients
  5. Irrigation: Copious saline irrigation (minimum 500ml), remove debris
  6. Debridement: Excise devitalized tissue, preserve vital structures
  7. Closure: Primary vs delayed based on contamination
  8. Dressing: Appropriate dressing based on wound type

Chronic Wound Types:

  • Pressure Ulcers: Stage I-IV, prevention is key (turn schedule)
  • Diabetic Foot Ulcers: Neuropathic + ischemic, multidisciplinary care
  • Venous Ulcers: Lower leg, stasis dermatitis, compression therapy
  • Arterial Ulcers: Painful, distal, poor pulses, revascularization needed
  • Marjolin's Ulcer: SCC in chronic wound, requires biopsy

Burn Care & Resuscitation

Burns are not just skin injuries - they are total body trauma! Remember the Rule of 9s, avoid ice, and always check for inhalation injury. Early and adequate fluid resuscitation saves lives. Monitor urine output - it's your window to perfusion!

Burn Pathophysiology

Etiology of Burns:

  • Thermal:
    • Scalds: Hot liquids (most common in children)
    • Flame burns: Direct fire contact, house fires
    • Contact: Hot metals, glass
  • Chemical: Acids or alkali, severity depends on concentration and duration
  • Electrical: Causes deep tissue damage, cardiac arrhythmias
  • Radiation: Sunburn, radiation therapy
  • Inhalation: Heat, smoke, toxic gases

Depth of Burn Injury:

Depth Layers Involved Appearance Sensation Healing Scarring
First Degree Epidermis only Erythema, no blisters Painful 3-5 days None
Superficial 2nd Epidermis + superficial dermis Red, blisters, moist Very painful 2-3 weeks Minimal
Deep 2nd Epidermis + deep dermis Pale, mottled, dry Less painful >3 weeks Yes, hypertrophy
Third Degree Full thickness (skin necrosis) White/charred, dry eschar Painless Never Always, requires grafting
Fourth Degree Into fascia, muscle, bone Charred, destroyed tissue Painless Never Amputation required

Histological Zones of Burn Injury:

  1. Zone of Coagulation: Central necrosis, irreversible tissue death
  2. Zone of Stasis: Intermediate zone, potentially salvageable with proper treatment
  3. Zone of Hyperemia: Outer zone, inflammatory response, reversible
Critical: The goal of burn treatment is to preserve the zone of stasis and prevent progression to coagulation through adequate perfusion and avoiding infection.

  • Particularly useful in children

Pathophysiology of Major Burns

Cardiovascular Changes:

  • Capillary Leak: Massive fluid loss in first 24-48 hours
    • Increased capillary permeability
    • Loss of plasma proteins into interstitium
    • Edema formation (wound and distant sites)
  • Hypovolemia: Third spacing leads to shock
  • Hematologic: Hemoconcentration, sludging, DIC risk
  • Cardiac Output: Initially decreased, later hyperdynamic

Metabolic Changes:

  • Hypermetabolism: 2-3x normal basal metabolic rate
  • Increased Energy Demand: 560 kcal lost per liter of water evaporated
  • Catabolism: Muscle breakdown, negative nitrogen balance
  • Temperature Regulation: Loss of skin barrier â†’ heat loss

Renal Changes:

  • Decreased renal blood flow (hypovolemia)
  • Acute tubular necrosis risk (myoglobinuria in electrical burns)
  • Oliguria common in early phase

Immunologic Changes:

  • Suppressed cell-mediated immunity
  • Neutrophil dysfunction
  • Increased susceptibility to infection

Respiratory Changes:

  • Upper airway edema (can obstruct)
  • Inhalation injury â†’ ARDS
  • Metabolic acidosis â†’ respiratory compensation
Key Point: For every 1% TBSA burn, basal metabolic rate increases by 10%. Adequate nutrition is critical!

Mechanism of Injury:

  • Heat: Upper airway edema, laryngeal spasm
  • Chemical: Smoke particles, toxic gases
  • Systemic: Carbon monoxide poisoning, cyanide poisoning

Management of Inhalation Injury:

  1. Airway Management:
    • Early intubation if any airway compromise
    • Fiberoptic laryngoscopy to assess edema
    • Cricothyroidotomy if cannot intubate
  2. 100% Oxygen:
    • Treat carbon monoxide poisoning
    • Reduces half-life of carboxyhemoglobin from 90 min to 30 min
  3. Bronchodilators: For bronchospasm
  4. Serial ABGs: Monitor for respiratory failure
  5. Mechanical Ventilation: If ARDS develops

  • Keep patient warm (prevent hypothermia)
  • Airway: Assess for inhalation injury

  • Circumferential deep burns of chest (restricts ventilation)
  • Circumferential burns of limbs (compromises circulation)
  • Signs: Tense eschar, decreased pulses, increased compartment pressure
  • Procedure: Incise through eschar only, not fascia

Special Burn Types

Chemical Burns:

  • Acids: Coagulative necrosis, self-limiting
  • Alkalis: Liquefactive necrosis (worse prognosis)
  • Treatment:
    • Remove contaminated clothing
    • Copious irrigation with water (minimum 30 minutes)
    • Identify chemical for specific antidotes
    • Hydrofluoric acid: Calcium gluconate gel

Electrical Burns:

  • Entry and exit wounds
  • Deep tissue damage (muscle necrosis)
  • Myoglobinuria â†’ renal failure
  • Cardiac arrhythmias
  • Compartment syndrome
  • Management:
    • Cardiac monitoring mandatory
    • Maintain urine output >2 mL/kg/hr
    • Alkalinize urine to prevent myoglobin precipitation
    • Fasciotomy for compartment syndrome

Cold Injury (Frostbite):

  • Classification similar to burns
  • Treatment: Rapid rewarming in warm water (40-42Â°C)
  • Avoid refreezing
Contraindicated in Burns: Ice application, topical anesthetics, butter/oils, popping blisters, tight dressings, prophylactic systemic antibiotics (without infection).

Critical Care & Resuscitation

Shock is not just low blood pressure! It's a life-threatening circulatory failure. Treat the cause, remember the lethal triad (Acidosis + Hypothermia + Coagulopathy), and always monitor urine output - it's your window to perfusion. Speed saves lives!

Definition & Pathophysiology

Definition:

Shock is a life-threatening condition of circulatory failure causing inadequate oxygen delivery to meet cellular metabolic needs, resulting in cellular and tissue hypoxia.

Three Components of Circulation (The Shock Triangle):

  • Blood Volume (Preload): Venous return to heart
  • Myocardial Contractility: Heart's pumping ability
  • Systemic Vascular Resistance: Afterload
Blood Pressure = Cardiac Output Ã— Peripheral Resistance

Cellular Effects of Shock:

  1. Compensated Phase: Catecholamine release, vasoconstriction
  2. Uncompensated Phase: Cellular hypoxia â†’ anaerobic metabolism
  3. Irreversible Phase: Cell death, organ failure

Hypovolemic Shock

Most Common Type of Shock!

Etiology:

  1. Blood Loss:
    • Trauma (hemorrhage)
    • Gastrointestinal bleed
    • Surgical bleeding
    • Postpartum hemorrhage
  2. Plasma Loss:
    • Burns (capillary leak)
    • Peritonitis (third spacing)
    • Pancreatitis
  3. Fluid & Electrolyte Loss:
    • Vomiting, diarrhea
    • GI fistulas
    • Diabetes insipidus

Pathophysiology - Compensatory Mechanisms:

  1. Neural Response:
    • Baroreceptor stimulation â†’ increased sympathetic tone
    • Tachycardia, vasoconstriction
    • Diverts blood to vital organs (brain, heart, kidneys)
  2. Endocrine Response:
    • Catecholamine release (epinephrine, norepinephrine)
    • Renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system activation
    • ADH secretion â†’ water retention
    • Cortisol, glucagon â†’ hyperglycemia
  3. Microcirculatory Changes:
    • Precapillary sphincter constriction
    • Sluggish blood flow â†’ stasis
    • Capillary leakage â†’ third spacing
Compensation: Up to 15% blood loss can be compensated by mechanisms above. Beyond 30%, compensation fails â†’ decompensated shock.

Anaphylactic & Neurogenic Shock

Anaphylactic Shock:

Life-threatening allergic reaction causing massive mast cell degranulation.

Triggers:

  • Drugs: Penicillin, cephalosporins, NSAIDs
  • Food: Peanuts, shellfish, eggs
  • Insect stings: Bees, wasps
  • Contrast media

Pathophysiology:

  1. Antigen-antibody (IgE) reaction
  2. Mast cell degranulation â†’ histamine release
  3. Effects:
    • Bronchospasm (airway obstruction)
    • Vasodilation (hypotension)
    • Increased capillary permeability (edema)
    • Urticaria, angioedema

Treatment (ABC Approach):

  1. Adrenaline 1:1000 IM: 0.3-0.5 mg (adult)
  2. Airway: Intubation if severe
  3. Breathing: High-flow Oâ‚‚, bronchodilators
  4. Circulation: IV fluids, vasopressors
  5. Steroids: Hydrocortisone 200 mg IV
  6. Antihistamines: Ranitidine + Diphenhydramine
  7. Observation: Biphasic reaction can occur up to 8 hours

Neurogenic Shock:

Loss of sympathetic tone â†’ vasodilation â†’ hypotension

Causes:

  • Spinal cord injury (above T6 - disrupts cardioaccelerator fibers)
  • High spinal anesthesia
  • Brainstem injury
  • Vasovagal syncope (transient)

Clinical Features:

  • Hypotension (SBP <90 mmHg)
  • Bradycardia (paradoxical)
  • Warm, dry skin (distributive mechanism)
  • Peripheral vasodilation
  • Priapism (spinal shock)

Treatment:

  1. Supine position, elevate limbs
  2. Fluid resuscitation (may be large volumes)
  3. Vasopressors: Norepinephrine, dopamine
  4. Atropine for bradycardia
  5. Maintain spinal precautions

Emergency Management Protocol

Primary Survey (ABCDE):

  1. A - Airway:
    • Assess patency, protect C-spine if trauma
    • Look for signs of obstruction
    • Establish definitive airway if needed
  2. B - Breathing:
    • Oxygen 100% by non-rebreather
    • Assess respiratory rate, pattern
    • Inspect chest movement, auscultate lungs
    • Treat tension pneumothorax immediately
  3. C - Circulation:
    • Check pulse, capillary refill
